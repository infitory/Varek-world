# The Old Rites

> **Type:** Folk practice / inherited tradition
> **Status:** Canonical

Not a religion, not a doctrine — a scattered inheritance of pre-collapse folk practice that persists throughout [Varek](../world/overview.md) in domestic and agricultural life.

Nearly every Varekian, regardless of their professed faith, practises some version of the Old Rites privately.

## Common practices

### Thresholds
- **Salt at the doorway** before a child's birth, a marriage, or a burial.
- **Iron under the step** to prevent what walks at night from walking into the house.
- **A thread tied at the lintel** when someone leaves on a long journey, to be untied only when they return.

### Crossroads
- **A coin left at a crossroads** before a journey. If the coin is there when you return, the journey was honest. If it is gone, something noticed you. If it has become two coins, you have been given permission for whatever comes next.
- **Speak no names at a crossroads after dusk.** The names may be heard.

### Birth
- **A word spoken over a newborn before the clergy name it.** The word varies by region, by family, by the woman attending. It is not the child's name. It is a protection the family knows.
- **The caul kept.** A child born with the caul is said to be lucky and vulnerable in equal measure. The caul is dried and kept in a small wooden box by the mother, sometimes for the child's entire life.

### The fields
- **A knot tied in the first furrow of spring.** Untied only when the harvest is in.
- **The last sheaf of the harvest** is not threshed with the rest. It is plaited, hung in the house, and burned on the shortest day of winter.
- **The first slice of bread from a new loaf** is set aside and left outside.

### The slaughter
- **A word spoken before a killing animal is killed.** Said quietly. Not a prayer in the Six-Faced sense. An acknowledgement.
- **The first blood** is not caught for sausage. It is allowed to fall into the earth.

### The dead
- **Coins on the eyes** — widespread. The denomination varies by region and wealth.
- **A window opened at the moment of death** to let whatever is leaving leave.
- **The deceased's shoes** turned upside-down for three nights after the death.
- **Certain words not spoken in the room where someone died for a full year.**

### The dark nights
- **A bowl of milk left out** on specific nights — the variation by region is extensive. Some leave it for the dead. Some leave it for "those who may come." Some simply leave it, and do not say why.
- **No weaving or sewing after dusk** on the nights that are known to be dangerous. Specific nights vary. The household knows.

## How the Rites are taught

The Old Rites are inherited — from mothers to daughters, grandmothers to granddaughters, sometimes from fathers to sons when a particular practice falls that way. They are taught by example. Children learn them by watching their elders and asking questions that are sometimes answered and sometimes not.

**There is no scripture.** There is no clergy. There is no central authority. Each household, each village, each region has its own inflection of the Rites. Some practices are nearly universal across Varek. Others are entirely local.

## The Six-Faced relationship

The clergy of [the Six-Faced God](six-faced-god.md) officially discourage the Old Rites and privately observe them when no one is watching.

- The **Provider** clergy are the most tolerant. Many Provider priests see the Rites as an older tradition that their faith subsumes and co-exists with. Some Provider priests practise particular Rites themselves and consider them compatible with their orders.
- The **Judge** clergy are the least tolerant. They regard the Rites as a parallel system of authority that erodes the proper structure of law and divinity.
- The **Architect** clergy are intellectually curious and politically cautious — they study the Rites in the archives, and publicly discourage them, and have been known to practise certain Rites in private.
- The **Wound** clergy regard the Rites as morally neutral — neither blessed nor condemned. The Wound's doctrine holds that suffering is universal and whatever helps a person endure it is, at worst, a tool.
- The **Quiet** clergy incorporate several Old Rites into their own practice without fanfare. The bowl of milk at the deathbed, the opened window, the upside-down shoes — these are all practices a Quiet priest may perform without considering them outside their faith.
- The **Unnamed** — unknown.

## What the Rites actually do

This is the uncomfortable part. **The Old Rites answer something.**

Sometimes. Not reliably. Not comprehensibly.

A coin left at a crossroads sometimes disappears and sometimes becomes two coins. A child's illness sometimes passes after the proper word is spoken. Most of the time, nothing happens. But *something* happens often enough to keep the practice alive — and, more disturbingly, **the response rate has been slowly increasing over the last several years.**

- More coins doubled.
- More illnesses passing after the proper word.
- More milk bowls found empty in the morning.
- More knots in the first furrow that hold through a bad season.

People have noticed. They are not, on balance, comforted. A practice that works *more often* than it used to is not a practice working better — it is a practice being answered by something that is paying more attention.

## The question no one asks aloud

**What is answering?**

The practice itself does not specify. The Rites invoke no named deity. They leave offerings to no particular addressee. They simply do — and sometimes something responds.

The [Drowned Mothers](buried-faiths.md#the-drowned-mothers) cultists claim the Rites are answered by the old river-goddesses. The adherents of the [Hollow King](buried-faiths.md#the-hollow-king) claim they are answered by the true god. The [Voiceless](voiceless.md) claim they are answered by the withheld voice of the Six-Faced God — that the Old Rites bypass the clergy and reach something that the clergy deliberately filter out.

The Architect clergy, in private, believe the Rites are residue from the [Old World](../history/old-world.md) — surviving practices that invoke something the Old World once organised and managed, which is now uncontrolled.

None of these explanations is provable. None is refutable. The Rites continue. The response rate continues to rise.

## Regional variation

A partial list of distinctive regional practices:

- **Ashfield Old Rites** — heavily focused on agriculture and weather. The first furrow knot, the last sheaf, the bread at threshold.
- **Greyspine Old Rites** — focused on stone and the underworld. Coins pressed into cracks in walls. Certain stones never moved, even when inconvenient. A handful of earth carried when a man goes underground for the first time.
- **Vorrland Old Rites** — the most elaborate, openly practised alongside Six-Faced observance. The Vorrland Rites include specific practices for the forest (words spoken when entering old groves, items left at the base of certain trees) that are taken seriously by everyone.
- **Siltmere Old Rites** — focused on water. The first catch of a fishing trip returned to the sea. A specific shell kept on the threshold of every fishing household. A coin thrown into a river before crossing it.
- **Veth Old Rites** — austere, heavily overlaid with Wound practice. The Veth variants are often indistinguishable from Wound devotions, and many Veth households would be surprised to learn that certain things they do are not part of the Six-Faced faith.

## Practitioners

There are no priests of the Old Rites. There are, however, **those who know more than others**.

- **The women who remember** — in most villages, one or two older women hold the majority of the local Rites in living memory. They are consulted when something has gone wrong in a way the clergy cannot address. They are not paid. They receive gifts.
- **The hedge-workers** (see [Magic and the Supernatural](../world/magic-and-supernatural.md#hedge-working)) — often overlap with the women who remember, but their practice is more active.
- **The bone-readers** — practitioners who read patterns in cast bones. An Old Rites technique. Occasionally accurate, for reasons no one has explained.

## The recent increase, in detail

Over the last five to seven years, the response rate of the Old Rites has approximately **doubled**. This is not a firm statistic — there is no census of Rite practice — but it is the subjective experience of older women across multiple regions who compare notes at markets.

Some specific reports:
- In an Ashfield village called **Merrith's Keep**, the milk bowl has been found empty every night for the last four months. This is unprecedented.
- In a Vorrland village near the [Vorrwood](../world/regions.md#the-vorrlands), the words spoken at the forest's edge are now being answered — in words, quietly, in a voice that sounds like the speaker's own. This has caused the village to stop speaking the words.
- In the [Siltmere](../kingdoms/siltmere.md), the first-catch return is being followed, within an hour, by an unusually large catch. Every time. Fishermen are grateful and worried.

Something is paying attention to Varek. Something that has always been there, quietly. Something that is now, for reasons no one understands, listening more closely.
