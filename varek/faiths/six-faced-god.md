# The Six-Faced God

> **Type:** Religion (dominant)
> **Status:** Canonical

The dominant religion of [Ostmere](../kingdoms/ostmere.md), [Veth](../kingdoms/veth.md), the [Siltmere](../kingdoms/siltmere.md), and the wealthier quarters of the [Free Cities](../kingdoms/free-cities.md).

## Core doctrine

There is **one god with six faces**, each representing an aspect of existence that must be honoured in its proper place. The faces are not equal. They compete. The resulting doctrine is internally contradictory in ways the clergy have elevated to a kind of art form.

Each face has its own order of clergy, its own scripture, its own authority, and its own quiet conviction that the other five faces are secondary or heretical. The orders cooperate in public ceremonies, fight over tithes in private, and have produced three schisms in the last two centuries that were papered over rather than resolved.

## Origin

The faith was codified during the [Long Reconstruction](../history/long-reconstruction.md), roughly three hundred years ago, from a synthesis of older regional traditions. Its founding documents are collectively known as the **Six Books**, each attributed to a different founding theologian and each associated with one face. Whether these theologians were historical figures, composites, or inventions is debated.

The faith spread from the south northward, serving as much as a unifying political instrument as a spiritual awakening.

## The Provider

> **Domain:** Harvest, labour, continuity, the mundane virtues
> **Clergy colour:** Pale green
> **Scripture:** *The Book of Common Days*
> **Temperament:** Patient, practical, quietly stubborn

Most rural chapels are Provider chapels. The faith of farmers, artisans, ordinary people.

### The Provider's clergy
The most numerous and the least powerful of the six orders. A typical Provider priest has a village, a small income, and an unglamorous life of blessing fields in spring, officiating at weddings, and burying people in autumn. Provider clergy are often married. They are allowed to hold property. They are the backbone of rural religious life across Ostmere.

### *The Book of Common Days*
Boring, beloved, and full of practical wisdom. Organised as a year's worth of daily readings, each pairing a verse with a short reflection on agricultural, domestic, or communal life. The book has been lightly revised in every generation; the current standard edition is the **Fourth Revised** (compiled eighty years ago).

### The Provider's paradox
The Provider is the face most immediately affected by the [Draining](../margins/the-draining.md). Harvests are diminishing. Livestock are smaller. The Provider's blessings appear less effective each year. The senior Provider clergy discuss this only among themselves, and not all of them agree on what it means.

## The Judge

> **Domain:** Law, judgement, the passage from life to death
> **Clergy colour:** Grey
> **Scripture:** *The Book of Measures*
> **Temperament:** Severe, precise, unyielding

Associated with courts, magistrates, and funerary rites. The Judge's clergy administer oaths, record inheritances, and preside over executions.

### The Judge's clergy
Feared rather than loved. Celibate. Cannot own property in their own name. Trained from a young age in law, memorisation, and the fine details of procedural ritual. A Judge priest at a trial is a sobering sight — they do not speak often, but when they do, the room listens.

Judge clergy also handle **the Weighing** — the formal ceremony conducted before and after major deaths, in which the deceased's life is summarised and a verdict is rendered on whether their affairs are in order. The verdict has legal weight: it confirms or invalidates wills, debts, marriage settlements.

### *The Book of Measures*
A dense legal text with theological commentary that almost no one outside the order fully understands. The Judge clergy spend decades studying it. There are said to be passages that only senior Judge priests are permitted to read.

### The Judge's current concern
The Judge clergy have noticed that the recent [ship-crew disappearances in the Siltmere](../margins/phenomena.md) have left the deceased's estates in a state that their laws do not adequately address. *Where there is no body and no confirmed death, the estate cannot be settled.* They are quietly proposing doctrinal adjustments to handle this. They do not yet realise how many such cases they will soon need to handle.

## The Architect

> **Domain:** Order, stone, structure, civilisation
> **Clergy colour:** Deep blue
> **Scripture:** *The Book of Foundations*
> **Temperament:** Intellectual, aloof, conservative

Historically the faith of kings and city-builders. The Architect's clergy are the intellectual elite of the Six-Faced faith.

### The Architect's clergy
Run libraries. Preserve the old histories. Teach the children of the nobility. Advise on construction, fortification, civic planning.

Many Architect clergy are scholars first and priests second. Their celibacy is less strict than the Judge's (they may marry with permission); their property rules are looser (they may hold personal libraries and small estates); their political influence is disproportionate to their numbers.

The **High Speaker of the Architect**, currently **Ormund Vale**, holds the Crown Voice position in Ostmere — the senior advisory seat to King Edvar III.

### *The Book of Foundations*
Includes cosmology, architecture, and a great deal of genealogy. The book is less a single text than a library — different chapters have different origins, different authorities, different levels of canonicity. Studying *Foundations* is a career.

### The Architect's archives
The Architect order maintains the largest institutional archives in Varek. Much of what is known about the [Old World](../history/old-world.md) is preserved in these archives — and much of what is not known is known by the Architect clergy to have been deliberately destroyed during [the Reclamation](../history/long-reconstruction.md#the-reclamation).

The Architect order is, quietly, the institution that knows the most about Varek's true history. It shares this knowledge sparingly.

## The Wound

> **Domain:** Suffering, endurance, the meaning found in pain
> **Clergy colour:** Rust-red
> **Scripture:** *The Book of the Cut*
> **Temperament:** Austere, intense, uncomfortable to be around

The most severe of the orders. Its priests are famous for taking vows of physical hardship — walking barefoot in winter, sleeping on stone, fasting beyond reason.

### The Wound's clergy
Celibate. Cannot own property. Eat only what is given to them, in quantities they did not choose. Their presence is respected and avoided in equal measure.

Wound clergy practise [scripture-binding](../world/magic-and-supernatural.md#scripture-binding) more openly than any other order, and consider the physical cost of the practice to be spiritually meaningful. The most experienced Wound priests have bodies that show the work — missing fingers from frost, cloudy eyes, tremors.

### *The Book of the Cut*
Short. Brutal. Quoted often. Its most famous passage, the **Seventeenth Cut**, is memorised by every Wound initiate and goes like this (paraphrased, in the tone of the faith):

*To live is to be pressed between two stones. The lower stone is the earth, and it does not move. The upper stone is grief, and it does not lift. Between them, the soul is ground into something finer than it was.*

### Where The Wound is strong
[Veth](../kingdoms/veth.md) — effectively the state religion, though this is not formally acknowledged.

The pilgrimage town of [Aster's Rest](../kingdoms/ostmere.md#asters-rest) in Ostmere, built around a minor relic.

The poorer districts of every major city. The Wound is the face of the suffering, and the suffering gravitate to it.

### The Wound's recent activity
The Wound clergy have quietly increased their activity in Ashfield villages. They bring supplies. They also ask questions: about dreams, about animals behaving strangely, about anyone who has recently returned changed from the forest.

The other orders have noticed this and are uncertain what it means.

## The Quiet

> **Domain:** Sleep, forgetting, the mercy of endings
> **Clergy colour:** Soft black-grey
> **Scripture:** *The Book of the Long Breath*
> **Temperament:** Calm, unsettling, oddly warm

Associated with hospice work, mourning, the care of the dying and the mad.

### The Quiet's clergy
Not celibate, but rarely marry. Permitted to own property, but rarely accumulate any. Serve in hospices, asylums, plague-houses, and any place where endings need to be witnessed well.

The Quiet clergy have a particular authority that the other orders lack — they are permitted to administer **the Last Breath**, a ritual dose of a specific herb-infusion that ends the life of a dying person who has requested it. This is legal, regulated, and rare. It is also a practice the other faces disagree with theologically and tolerate politically.

### *The Book of the Long Breath*
Partly liturgy, partly a practical guide to dying well. Includes instructions for preparation, passages to be read to the dying, and considerable material on grief and its management. One of the most widely read Six-Faced texts outside the clergy — ordinary Varekians often own a private copy for use when needed.

### The Quiet's reputation
Tolerated rather than celebrated. The other orders find them unsettling, but nobody wishes to abolish them, because everyone eventually needs them.

> *"When the Quiet comes to the door, you let them in. You do not have to like them."* — Ostmere saying

## The Unnamed

> **Domain:** Unknown / unspoken
> **Clergy colour:** None publicly known
> **Scripture:** None publicly known
> **Temperament:** Absent

The sixth face. **No one at the senior levels of the Six-Faced clergy will publicly name it or describe its aspect.**

### Official doctrine
The Unnamed is the face of mystery — the aspect of the god that humanity is not permitted to know. To speak its aspect aloud is to diminish it. The six faces are commonly invoked as "The Provider, The Judge, The Architect, The Wound, The Quiet, and the One Whose Face is Turned."

### Unofficial followers
Minor unofficial followers exist in every city — often among the educated, often among the marginal. They meet privately. They practise a liturgy that the mainstream clergy consider blasphemous but cannot quite prove is heretical. Their private doctrine holds that the Unnamed has a name, a face, and a specific domain, and that these will be revealed at a particular time.

### Recent developments
In the last year, the unofficial followers of the Unnamed have **gone almost entirely silent**.

- Their private meetings have ceased in most cities.
- The small shrines to the Unnamed that existed in certain private households have been quietly dismantled.
- Adherents who were once willing to discuss the face privately now refuse.
- A few have left their homes without explanation.

No one at the senior clergy will comment on what this means. The [Voiceless](voiceless.md) regard the silence of the Unnamed's followers as significant in a way they will not fully articulate.

> *"When the Unnamed pulls back, either it knows something or it has been called."* — attributed to a Quiet abbess, disputed.

## The High Council of Faces

The formal governing body of the Six-Faced faith. Composed of the **High Speakers** of each face (for the Unnamed, a seat exists but is traditionally left empty). Meets four times a year. Its decisions have theological weight across all four polities that practise the faith.

The High Council is currently in a state of quiet tension. The Provider speaker is worried about the harvests. The Judge speaker is worried about the estate-settlement problem. The Architect speaker (Ormund Vale) is distracted by Ostmere politics. The Wound speaker is conducting her own investigations and sharing nothing. The Quiet speaker is the oldest and says very little. The seat for the Unnamed is empty, as always.

The next Council meeting is in six weeks. It will be one of the most consequential in a century, though most of the attendees do not yet know this.

## Schisms

Three major schisms have occurred in the last two centuries:

1. **The Southern Schism** (one hundred and eighty years ago) — a Siltmere-originating movement that tried to elevate The Provider above the other faces. Lasted forty years. Papered over by compromise rather than resolved.
2. **The Winter Schism** (ninety-two years ago) — a Veth movement that tried to formally elevate The Wound. Formally resolved; informally, Veth continues to practise something close to what the schism proposed.
3. **The Silent Schism** (thirty-one years ago) — a brief, strange movement that wanted to formally remove the Unnamed from the faith entirely. Collapsed quickly. Its leaders left the clergy and disappeared into private life. Two of them are now known to have been among the unofficial followers of the Unnamed. The others are presumed dead or elsewhere.
