# The First Collapse

> **Type:** History (cataclysmic event)
> **Period:** Approximately 400 years ago, spanning 30-50 years
> **Status:** Canonical

The event around which all Varekian history organises itself. The end of the [Old World](old-world.md).

## Duration

The First Collapse was not a single event. It occurred over the course of approximately **thirty to fifty years** — accounts differ.

- **The Early Collapse** (first decade or so) — a period of escalating disasters, but within the Old World's institutional capacity to manage.
- **The Great Collapse** (middle period, perhaps twenty years) — the catastrophic core of the event, during which the Old World's institutions failed and its population was catastrophically reduced.
- **The Tail** (final decade or so) — a period of quiet extinction, during which the remnants of Old World civilisation finished dying and the survivors began the process that would become the [Long Silence](long-silence.md).

## What happened

The surviving records — most of them written decades later, by people working from damaged sources — describe:

### Sudden plagues

Plagues that killed **without pattern**. Not a single disease but multiple diseases appearing in quick succession. Some of the diseases appeared to be novel — not present in earlier records. Others were familiar diseases behaving in unfamiliar ways: striking in seasons they had never struck before, affecting populations they had previously spared.

Modern scholars have identified, in the surviving records, at least five distinct plague events of the Early and Great Collapse periods. There may have been more.

### Urban emptyings

**Cities whose populations vanished overnight.**

The most famous — and best documented — is the account of **Elvoram**, an Old World city whose ruins lie beneath what is now the southern [Ashfields](../world/regions.md#the-ashfields). An Old World official arriving at Elvoram found the city "whole, and empty of breath" — buildings intact, goods in shops, meals half-eaten, no bodies, no signs of violence.

Elvoram is not unique. The records describe at least seven similar emptyings, across a span of several years. The phenomenon is **the clearest parallel** to the recent [empty villages of the Vorrwood](../margins/phenomena.md) — a parallel the [High Chapter of Dusk's Edge](../kingdoms/free-cities.md#dusks-edge) does not yet know to draw.

### The failure of the Great Magic

At some point in the Great Collapse, **the Great Magic of the Old World stopped working.** Not gradually. According to the records, within a period of perhaps three years, the civic-scale enchantments that the Old World depended on ceased to function.

Cities lost their self-healing. Roads began to decay. Water systems failed. Lighting failed. The ambient magic that had saturated Old World life simply **went out**.

What is not clear: whether the Great Magic failed because its practitioners died, or whether its practitioners died because the Great Magic failed, or whether both were consequences of the same deeper cause.

### The emergence of new things

**Things that had not been seen before, and could not be readily named, appeared in the Old World during the Collapse.**

The records describe them in varied terms. Some sound like what the modern era calls [hollow-men](../margins/creatures.md) or [night-calves](../margins/creatures.md). Others sound like nothing currently known. Entities are described in the records as:

- "Walking but not of flesh."
- "Speaking in voices that were never heard before."
- "Present in many places at once, in smaller numbers at each."
- "Knowing things they should not have known."

The descriptions are consistent across multiple independent sources — a fact scholars consider significant. Whatever appeared, it appeared across the continent, and multiple observers noted similar features.

### The darkening

Some records describe a **darkening of the sky** during the Collapse — a period of extended overcast, reduced sunlight, and unusual cold.

The duration given varies: two years, seven years, eighteen years. Modern scholars believe some kind of extended climatic anomaly did occur, probably lasting a few years rather than decades. The darkening is sometimes called **the Long Shadow** in later texts.

### The long silence that followed

By the end of the Collapse, the records simply **stop**. This is not because nothing was happening — something was happening, because the reduced population that survived was still present and still living. But **almost no one was writing**. Literacy collapsed. Institutions collapsed. The mechanisms that produced records ceased to function.

The century that followed is the [Long Silence](long-silence.md). Very little is known about it.

## Causes: the four theories

Modern scholars argue about what caused the First Collapse. Four major theories are recognised.

### The Exhaustion Theory

The Old World **drained the world of some vital substance**, and when it ran out, everything dependent on it collapsed. The Great Magic required some form of fuel — a resource, a quality, an energy — that accumulated slowly and was consumed by use. Its depletion caused the cascade.

This theory is **favoured by most secular historians**. It has the advantage of not requiring any supernatural agents beyond the failure of existing ones. It also accounts for the ongoing [Draining](../margins/the-draining.md) as the continued — perhaps accelerating — effect of the same depletion.

Problems with the theory: it does not explain the appearance of new entities during the Collapse, nor the urban emptyings, which were not obviously related to the failure of magic.

### The Punishment Theory

The Old World **committed some unforgivable act** against the gods or the land, and was punished.

This theory is **favoured by the Six-Faced clergy**, particularly the orders of [The Judge](../faiths/six-faced-god.md#the-judge) and [The Wound](../faiths/six-faced-god.md#the-wound). Judge doctrine frames the Collapse as a cosmic verdict. Wound doctrine treats it as an instructive suffering, whose lessons the current era is meant to learn.

The theory has the advantage of explaining the Collapse as meaningful rather than merely unfortunate. It also provides a framework for the current religious order — the Six-Faced God emerged, in the [Long Reconstruction](long-reconstruction.md), partly as a response to the Punishment.

Problems with the theory: it does not specify what the unforgivable act was, and different Six-Faced orders propose different candidates.

### The Intrusion Theory

**Something entered Varek from elsewhere** and consumed the Old World.

This theory is favoured by certain marginal scholars and by [the Voiceless](../faiths/voiceless.md). It holds that the Collapse was not caused by internal failure but by an external agent — a force, a presence, an entity or group of entities — that arrived in Varek and systematically unmade its civilisation.

The theory accounts for the appearance of new entities and for the urban emptyings. It does not easily account for the failure of the Great Magic, unless one argues that the Great Magic was broken by the intrusion specifically.

The theory is considered a fringe view by most academic historians. It is taken more seriously in certain quiet parts of the [Architect order](../faiths/six-faced-god.md#the-architect). It is also the theory that most closely describes what the [Hollow King](../faiths/buried-faiths.md#the-hollow-king) heresy believes — though the Hollow King heresy inverts the moral: what intruded, they say, was not an enemy but a returning god.

### The Suicide Theory

**The Old World destroyed itself, deliberately**, for reasons that are no longer clear.

This theory is favoured by a small minority of scholars who have read the fragments other scholars will not read. It holds that some faction within the Old World — perhaps its ruling class, perhaps a religious movement, perhaps a scholarly elite — came to the conclusion that the Old World **had to end**, and took steps to end it.

The fragments these scholars cite include: texts from the final decades of the Old World that describe "the necessary ending"; ritual records that appear to describe large-scale sacrifices whose purpose is not specified; architectural modifications to certain major structures that seem to have been made for the purpose of **destruction** rather than use.

The theory is disturbing enough that most scholars will not engage with it. Those who do are often quietly discouraged from continuing.

## Which theory is correct

**It is possible all four are correct.**

- The Great Magic exhausted itself.
- The Old World's moral failures accumulated into a cosmic reckoning.
- Something entered Varek from elsewhere during the Old World's moment of weakness.
- A faction within the Old World saw what was coming and hastened the end, or tried to shape it.

There is no evidence that these theories are mutually exclusive. The Collapse was a many-layered event. Each theory may describe a different layer.

No theory is accepted. All four are partially supported by surviving evidence. The scholarly consensus, to the extent there is one, is **uncomfortable agnosticism**.

## Survivors

The First Collapse reduced the Old World's population to perhaps a tenth of its peak — possibly less. The survivors were **not a representative sample** of the Old World's population. They were:

- Disproportionately from rural areas, which the urban emptyings had not touched in the same way.
- Disproportionately from regions the new entities had, for reasons unknown, less interest in.
- Disproportionately from among those who had already been outside the Old World's mainstream — hermits, exiles, religious dissidents, poor populations that had been ignored.

This selection effect shaped the Long Silence and the Long Reconstruction. Modern Varek descends not from the Old World's cosmopolitan core but from its margins. This may be why so much Old World knowledge was lost — the margins were never the knowledge-keepers in the first place.

## The connection to the present

The First Collapse is, to most Varekians, **ancient history**. Something that happened to their distant ancestors. Not a living concern.

Certain people in Varek — a small number of senior clergy, a handful of scholars, a portion of the Voiceless, the priestesses of the [Drowned Mothers](../faiths/buried-faiths.md#the-drowned-mothers), the older women who hold the [Old Rites](../faiths/old-rites.md) — are beginning to notice that the **signs** of the current era have parallels with accounts of the Early Collapse.

Not identical. But similar enough to make certain people uneasy.

None of these observers has yet connected with the others. Each is noticing privately, in their own domain, something that resembles a pattern they have read about.

They will begin to connect soon. The question is whether they will do so in time for it to matter.
