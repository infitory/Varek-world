# The Veth Wars

> **Type:** History (recent conflict)
> **Period:** 80-60 years ago
> **Status:** Canonical

Three wars between [Ostmere](../kingdoms/ostmere.md) and [Veth](../kingdoms/veth.md) over the northern passes of the [Greyspine](../world/regions.md#the-greyspine), fought eighty, seventy-four, and sixty years ago respectively.

The last and longest — **the Sixteen-Year War** — ended with Veth's repulsion and a treaty that has held, nominally, for sixty years.

## The First Veth War

> **Years ago:** 80
> **Duration:** ~2 years
> **Outcome:** Stalemate; status quo ante

A brief border conflict triggered by a succession dispute in one of the smaller lordships of the northern Ashfields. Both sides intervened. Neither gained meaningful ground. The war ended with a treaty that restored the pre-war borders and required Veth to pay a small indemnity.

The First War is mostly a footnote in modern military histories. Its significance is that it **established the pattern** — periodic, inconclusive Ostmere-Veth conflict — that would continue for generations.

### Notable figures

- **King Harren II** of Ostmere — the grandfather of the current King Edvar. Considered competent. Died of natural causes after the war.
- **Prince-Marshal Serak Veth II** — grandfather of the current Prince-Marshal. Lost the war but consolidated his internal power through the conflict.

## The Second Veth War

> **Years ago:** 74
> **Duration:** ~3 years
> **Outcome:** Marginal Ostmere victory; minor territorial adjustments

Six years after the First War, Veth attempted a more serious incursion, targeting **Hangfall Pass** — the primary route between the two kingdoms. Veth forces captured the pass and held it for eighteen months before an Ostmere counter-offensive drove them back.

The Second War established [Kolven](../kingdoms/veth.md#kolven) as a contested site — it changed hands twice during this war.

### Notable figures

- **King Edvar I** of Ostmere — father of Edvar II, grandfather of the current Edvar III. A more capable king than his son would be.
- **Prince-Marshal Serak Veth II** (still) — led Veth through both wars, died three years after the Second War's end.
- **General Marrik Drost** — Ostmere's senior military commander in the Second War. Distant ancestor of [Lord Caen Drost](../kingdoms/ostmere.md#drosts-fief), [Aldric's](../characters/aldric.md) former commander.

## The Third Veth War (The Sixteen-Year War)

> **Years ago:** 60 (ended)
> **Duration:** 16 years
> **Outcome:** Veth repulsion; current treaty

The longest, bloodiest, and most consequential of the three wars.

### Cause

Prince-Marshal **Serak Veth III** (father of the current Serak IV) inherited a principality with a strong military tradition and a weakening treasury. His advisors proposed an ambitious solution: **full annexation of northern Ostmere**, specifically the grain-producing regions of the upper Ashfields. This would solve Veth's food problem permanently.

The plan was bold, possibly desperate, and initially successful.

### The war itself

The Sixteen-Year War unfolded in three broad phases:

#### Phase One: The Veth Advance (Years 1-4)

Veth forces crossed Hangfall Pass in early spring and advanced into northern Ostmere with surprising speed. Within the first year, they had captured:

- **Kolven** (easily — Veth considered it effectively theirs anyway)
- **The fortress of Wessen's Rest** (after a four-month siege)
- Several border towns in the upper Ashfields

Ostmere was slow to respond. King Edvar II was not a good wartime king. The standing army was smaller than Veth's, and the levies took time to raise.

By the end of Year Four, Veth had established forward positions as far south as a line approximately forty miles north of what is now the garrison town of [Dren](../kingdoms/ostmere.md#dren).

#### Phase Two: The Grinding (Years 5-12)

The Veth advance halted. Ostmere's levies arrived in force. The terrain of the upper Ashfields — flat, open, and easily fortified — favoured the defender.

The middle phase of the war became **attritional trench warfare across a static front**. Both sides dug in. Both sides raided. Both sides bled.

The Grinding is the part of the war that produced most of its horrors:

- **The Burning of Harren's Mill** (Year 6) — a Veth raid that killed perhaps three hundred civilians, including an incident in a granary that became, in the post-war imagination, emblematic of the war's brutality.
- **The Winter of Black Bread** (Year 8) — a failed harvest in Ostmere that nearly broke civilian morale. The army ate. The civilians did not always.
- **The Plague Camp** (Year 9) — a Veth winter encampment that developed a severe illness, which spread to nearby Ostmere positions. Both sides lost troops and blamed each other. Modern scholars believe it was a natural outbreak of what was probably a variant of the still-cough.
- **The Long March** (Year 11) — an Ostmere attempt at a flanking manoeuvre through the Greyspine that went badly wrong. Of roughly two thousand men sent, fewer than four hundred returned. The survivors walked for seven weeks through winter mountains without adequate supplies.

#### Phase Three: The Veth Collapse (Years 13-16)

By Year 13, Veth's treasury was exhausted and its population strained. Prince-Marshal Serak III was in failing health. His son (the current Serak IV, then a young commander) argued for a negotiated peace. His advisors insisted on continuing.

In Year 14, Ostmere forces under a new commander — **General Vannis Therrick**, the current Lord Marshal of Ostmere, who was then a young officer — launched a sustained offensive that recaptured Wessen's Rest and pushed Veth back across Hangfall Pass.

The war ended in Year 16 with the **Treaty of Hestrom Stone**, signed at a neutral site in the eastern Greyspine. The treaty:

- Restored the pre-war border.
- Required Veth to pay an indemnity of one hundred thousand silver marks (never fully paid; the debt persists in various forms).
- Established a permanent joint commission to arbitrate border disputes.
- Required Veth to demilitarise a narrow strip of territory along the Ostmere border (a provision Veth has quietly violated for forty years without formal Ostmere complaint).

### Notable figures

- **King Edvar II** of Ostmere — a poor wartime king who improved slightly as the war dragged on. Died in his sleep two years after the war's end.
- **Prince-Marshal Serak Veth III** — led Veth into the war, died in Year 15 of illness, leaving his son to negotiate the peace.
- **General Vannis Therrick** — the architect of Ostmere's final offensive. Now Lord Marshal of Ostmere, aged but still serving.
- **Captain Orren Drost** — Lord Caen Drost's uncle. Killed at the Burning of Harren's Mill, defending the granary. A family legend that Aldric heard many times during his service under Lord Drost.
- **Aldric's grandfather, Hallen** — fought in the final two years of the Grinding. Died of wounds received at a minor engagement at a place called **Three Oaks Crossing**. Aldric's father was four years old at the time.
- **Aldric's father, Vorrell** — too young to fight in the war itself. Fought in the skirmishes that continued for a decade after the treaty, as the border slowly settled. Survived, but carried a limp from a wound taken at age twenty-two. Died of the still-cough twelve years ago, which Aldric has always understood as a premonition of his own fate.

## The aftermath

The Sixteen-Year War shaped modern Varek in ways that persist.

### Cultural effects

- **The Mourning Walk** — Ostmere's annual commemoration for the war dead, held every late summer. The walk traces a specific route from Varrentholm to the battlefield at Hessford, where the largest single battle of the war was fought. Aldric has walked it eleven times.
- **The Winter of Black Bread** entered folk memory as a reference point for hardship. Older Ostmere people still say "a winter black as the bread" to mean a very bad winter.
- **War-widows and war-orphans** remained a significant demographic category for a generation after the war. Many settled in the outer wards of Varrentholm, contributing to the overcrowding that persists today.

### Political effects

- The current pattern of Ostmere-Veth relations — formal peace, constant low-grade tension, periodic border incidents — was established by the post-war settlement and has not fundamentally changed.
- The **standing army of Ostmere** grew out of the war's mobilisation and has never fully demobilised. The permanent six thousand men along the Veth border are, in a sense, still fighting the war — or rather, still preparing for its resumption.
- Lord Marshal Therrick's generation of veterans dominated Ostmere's military leadership for decades. Aldric's generation is, broadly, their students — trained by men who had fought in the war, under doctrines shaped by it.

### Economic effects

- Veth has **never recovered** from the war's costs. The hundred-thousand-mark indemnity was renegotiated into various trade concessions, debt arrangements, and informal obligations that still shape Veth's economic situation. The current Prince-Marshal's financial problems are, in part, the long tail of his father's defeat.
- Ostmere emerged economically weakened but politically stronger. The war consolidated the crown's authority — the lords had needed the crown to coordinate the war, and the crown had needed the lords to fund it. The post-war settlement strengthened both.

### The veterans

The war produced roughly fifteen thousand Ostmere veterans who survived. Of those, perhaps two thousand are still alive — old men now, in their seventies and eighties. They are the last living connection to the war.

Aldric's father was of the generation that **grew up on the war without fighting its main battles**. Aldric is of the generation that **grew up on the stories** and joined the standing army expecting a fourth war that has not yet come.

The fourth war is now becoming more likely, not less. Prince-Marshal Serak Veth IV — the man who argued against his father's war as a young commander — is now considering whether to start the next one himself.

## The fourth war that has not come

Sixty years of peace have made the Sixteen-Year War feel like ancient history to most people in Ostmere and Veth. It is not. The men who fought it are still alive. Their grievances still exist. The geography that made it worth fighting — the Hangfall Pass, the upper Ashfields, the Greyspine's grain and iron — is still there, still contested, still producing reasons for conflict.

What has changed is that the kingdoms are now both **weaker** than they were at the start of the previous war. Ostmere's crown is unsteady. Veth's treasury is ruined. The [Draining](../margins/the-draining.md) has reduced both populations. The [Six-Faced clergy](../faiths/six-faced-god.md) are distracted by theological strains they will not publicly acknowledge.

A fourth Veth War, if it comes, will not resemble the first three. It will be shorter, because neither kingdom can sustain a long conflict. It will be more desperate, because both sides know they cannot afford to lose. And it will occur against the backdrop of **something else going wrong in Varek** — something no one in either capital has yet fully noticed.

A war between Ostmere and Veth right now would be the worst possible response to the actual crisis that is beginning. It is, for that reason, becoming increasingly likely.
